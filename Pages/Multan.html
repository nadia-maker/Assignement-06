    Multan History

    Multan, one of the oldest cities in the Indian subcontinent, is known for its rich history, vibrant culture,
    and deep religious significance. It is often referred to as the "City of Saints" due to the large number of Sufi shrines and saints buried here.

    Ancient History

    • Multan’s history dates back over 5,000 years. It was an important center for the Indus Valley Civilization and later became part of the Vedic civilization.

    • According to ancient texts, Multan was known as Mulasthana and was famous for the Sun Temple dedicated to the Hindu sun god, Surya.

    • The city was mentioned by Greek historians like Herodotus and Ptolemy. Alexander the Great captured Multan in 326 BCE after a fierce battle with the Mallian people.

      • Medieval Period

    • Multan was conquered by the Arabs in 712 CE under Muhammad bin Qasim, who introduced Islam to the region. It remained an important center of trade, learning, and religion under Muslim rule.

    • During the Ghaznavid and Ghurid periods (11th-12th centuries), Multan became a hub of Islamic scholarship.

    • The city was ruled by various Muslim dynasties, including the Delhi Sultanate and the Mughal Empire, under which it flourished economically and culturally.

      • Sufi Influence and Saints

    • Multan is famous for being a major center of Sufism. Some of the most renowned saints buried here include:

    • Bahauddin Zakariya

    • Shah Rukn-e-Alam

    • Shah Shams Tabrez

      • These shrines continue to attract thousands of devotees every year.

      Colonial and Modern Era

    • The Sikhs captured Multan in 1818 under Ranjit Singh, but British forces later took control in 1849.

    • After Pakistan’s independence in 1947, Multan became a key city in Punjab and has grown into a major agricultural and commercial center.
